How Do People Living with Mental Illnesses Experience
Mental Health Stigma in TV and Movies?

An Honor's 202 Project by Brittani Wallsten

Introduction

Mental illness is a common theme in movies and tv, specifically in the thriller and horror genres.
But how do people living with severe and persistent mental illness (SPMI) experience these stereotypes
and stigma? This project aims to explore some of the experiences and feelings of mentally ill individuals
brought on by mass media presentation of mental illness. Research in this area would be an important addition
to various fields of psychology, public health, and social stigma research and could inform treatment providers
directly.

Background

There have been many studies about the stigma surrounding mental illness and how it is perpetuated by the media.
These studies show that the way mental illness is portrayed in mass media has a broadly negative effect on public
attitudes both generally and personally (Kimmerle & Cress, 2013; Wahl, 1992). There are also several studies about
how self-stigma significant consequences for people can have living with mental illness (Corrigan, Watson, & Barr, 2006;
Livingston & Boyd, 2010; Colizzi, Ruggeri, & Lasalvia, 2020). There are far fewer studies done on how these stigmas and
stereotypes in the media are experienced by mentally ill individuals themselves (Stuart, 2006).

Rationale

This study seeks to explore how people living with severe mental health diagnoses experience the portrayal of mental
illness on screen. Many people with SPMI are isolated from the public by family, houselessness, or residential and
inpatient treatment programs. What impacts might this limited access and distorted presentations from the mass media
have on their sense of self and place in society? How might negative self-perception affect treatment outcomes and
overall wellbeing? And what do individuals see as areas of challenge or hope? The results of this study would have
implications broadly for mass media, mental healthcare, and health communication. It could encourage media creators,
activists, treatment providers, public health officials, etc. to pay closer attention to how mental illness is showing up
in TV and movies, and hopefully to advocate for more accurate/positive representation.

Methods

  1. The primary method of this study will be a focus group discussion with photo and film elicitation. The group will
    last about an hour and will be in a comfy private sitting area. Photos or short clips of popular movies with a
    mentally ill character will be shown and then a facilitated discussion will follow. The facilitators will ask things
    like “How would you describe this character? How do you think the other character’s feel about them? What do you think
    should be done to/for this person? Do you think the director got something wrong about this character? How would you feel
    if you were this character? Etc.”
    • Conversations will be recorded and transcribed and the facilitators will record people’s affect, engagement, mood presentation,
      and any changes that occur during the conversation.
    • The participants will be English-speaking adults with a mental health disability according to the Social Security Administration.
    • There would be 20-30 people in total with 4-5 per groups.
  2. The secondary method will The Self-Stigma in Mental Illness Scale (SSMIS) as used in Corrigan et al., 2016.
    • This survey would be given before and after the primary method to have a baseline and see if reports change based on the conversation.
    • The conversations and observations will be coded and analyzed to see how people experience the photos, clips, and discussion
